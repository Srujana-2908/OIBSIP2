Tribute to Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan

About Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan

Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan (1888-1975) was an Indian philosopher, statesman, and the second President of India (1962-1967). He was a prominent scholar and advocate of education and served as the Vice-Chancellor of Andhra University and Banaras Hindu University. Radhakrishnan played a crucial role in bridging the gap between Eastern and Western philosophies and promoting Indian culture internationally.

Education

Radhakrishnan's educational journey began at the Christian Missionary Society High School in Tirupati, followed by Voorhees College in Vellore. He then pursued his higher education at Madras Christian College, where he excelled in philosophy. In 1909, he obtained his master's degree in philosophy, topping the university. He went on to teach philosophy at various educational institutions, including Madras Presidency College, University of Mysore, and Calcutta University.

Key Contributions

  • Promoted the philosophy of Advaita Vedanta

  • Advocated for the importance of education and served as an influential teacher

  • Represented India at international forums and played a significant role in shaping India's foreign policy

  • Received numerous accolades, including the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian award, in 1954

Awards

Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan received numerous awards and honors for his distinguished work. In 1954, he was awarded the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian award. He also received honorary degrees from several universities around the world, including the University of Oxford, Cambridge University, and the University of Chicago.

Quotes

"Teachers should be the best minds in the country."

"The true teachers are those who help us think for ourselves."

"When we think we know, we cease to learn."

Death

Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan passed away on April 17, 1975, but his ideas and contributions continue to inspire generations of scholars, philosophers, and educators. He remains a revered figure in Indian history and a symbol of intellectual excellence and cultural diplomacy.
